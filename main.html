Computational Chemistry

1927: the first theoretical calculations in chemistry were made by Walter Heitler and Fritz Wolfgang London using valence bond theory.
1940s: development of more efficient computer technology → solving elaborate wave equations for complex atomic systems feasible
1964: Hückel method calculations for molecules like butadiene, benzene, and ovalene were generated on computers at Berkeley and Oxford
1970: The term "computational chemistry" was mentioned in the book "Computers and Their Role in the Physical Sciences" by Fernbach and Taub

Quantum Computing

Brief history of Quantum Computing

  • 1982: Starts with Richard Feynman lectures on the potential advantages of quantum computing
  • 1985: The idea of a “universal quantum computer”
  • 1996: A quantum algorithm which can simulate quantum-mechanical systems
  • 2001: the first implementation of an algorithm that factored 15 into its prime factors on a 7-qubit processor

Quantum Computing

  • Quantum computers use Q-bits. These are often electrons but can also be other things such as photons
  • Electrons can be “on” or “off” like a classical bit in that they can spin up or spin down in a field
  • But the difference from a classical bit is that quantum effects apply to them and they can actually be in a weighted superposition of being both spin up and spin down
  • As more Q-bits are added, this creates something different from classical computers- in a classical computer, within N bits, N pieces of information are contained in that each of them can be on or off
  • Entanglement:Two particles being entangled with each other implies that if the state of one of them collapses to a certain value, the state of the other can instantly be known, no matter how far separated they are from each other in space. “spooky action at a distance”
  • Quantum computers need to be kept at a very low temperature, and in fact most of the volume that a quantum computer takes up is its cooling apparatus
  • Also prone to error

Classic Computing

  • A classical computer is made up of “bits” which are really electrical switches that can be turned on or off. They are definitively 0 or 1 with no in between
  • Classical computing works in Binary, therefore it is in base 2
  • A 4 bit aggregation is a nybble
  • Capital A has been standardized as the number 65 or 01000001
  • The highest number you can count to with 8 bits is 255 but 256 digits are represented(0-255)
  • Kilobytes - thousand of bytes, Megabytes - millions of bytes etc.

Applications of quantum computing in Computational Chemistry and Challenges

  • Quantum computers are able to solve certain types of chemistry problems a lot better than classical computers
  • Specifically, quantum computers should be able to handle exponentially larger and more complex calculations, like those needed to solve the Schrodinger equation, because the qubits that make them up take advantage of quantum states.
  • Unlike binary digits, or bits, made up of ones and zeros, qubits can exist in two states simultaneously
  • On an optimized quantum computer, however, the time increases polynomially, not linearly, This means, not shockingly, that quantum computers will take significantly less time than classical, and that this difference in time increases with more complicated problems.

Typical Job Functions

Examples of work a computational chemist may perform include:

  • Using simulations to identify sites on protein molecules that are most likely to bind a new drug molecule
  • Creating models of synthesis reactions to demonstrate the effects of kinetics and thermodynamics.
  • Exploring the basic physical processes underlying phenomena such as superconductivity, energy storage, corrosion, or phase changes.

Typical work duties of a computational chemist include:

  • Applying new software and hardware capabilities for data collection and analysis
  • Developing computer models and simulations of chemical and biochemical processes and entitie
  • Performing and interpreting statistical analysis of large datasets
  • Creating visual representations of reaction pathways, molecular interactions, or other phenomena
  • Characterizing new compounds and processes to support patent claims
  • Helping to develop synthesis processes
  • Providing customer service or sales support
  • Teaching courses and training students
  • Designing experiments

Career Paths

  • Industry employers typically include companies in the pharmaceutical, petroleum, and industrial chemistry areas.
  • Government jobs are available at the national laboratories and various government agencies.
  • Computational chemists in academic environments often teach courses or provide individualized instruction on using various types of software or data analysis. At national laboratories, they may train visiting users, and they may perform their own research.
  • Customer service computational chemists may travel to their customers' laboratories to provide them with training or technical assistance.
